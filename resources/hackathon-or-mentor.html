Find a Hackathon or Mentor

Back to Hub
Leadership 6–7 min read

Competitions and mentors accelerate learning. The right hackathon builds teamwork and focus; the right mentor helps you aim high and avoid common mistakes. This guide shows Abu Dhabi students how to find opportunities, reach out respectfully, and turn experiences into lasting growth.

Photo placeholder — student team hacking with sticky notes and laptops
Clear roles, short sprints, and a simple demo beat over‑ambitious plans.

Part 1 — Find a hackathon that fits

1) Clarify your goal

Pick one objective for your next event: learn a new skill, build your portfolio, or meet collaborators. Your goal shapes which event to choose and how you prepare.

2) Where to search

  • School and university calendars; ask ICT and science departments.
  • Regional challenges and youth innovation programs.
  • Online portals (e.g., Devpost) and local community meetups.

3) Prepare a lightweight team toolkit

Agree on roles (lead, builder, designer, presenter), set a shared folder, and define a simple scope. Aim for a focused demo that solves one problem well.

Diagram placeholder — problem → prototype → test → pitch
A tight loop helps you ship a working demo in 24–48 hours.

4) Pitch and present clearly

Use a three‑part structure: problem, solution, impact. Show a demo, not just slides. Share honest limitations and next steps to stand out.

Part 2 — Find and work with a mentor

1) Identify your learning gap

Write two sentences: what you’re building and where you’re stuck. Mentors respond best to specific, realistic requests, not “teach me everything.”

2) Who to approach

  • Teachers and school alumni working in tech, sustainability, or design.
  • Local professionals and partner organizations engaged in community projects.
  • University students who can offer near‑peer guidance.

3) Craft a respectful message

Keep it short: who you are, what you’re making, your specific question, and 2–3 time windows that work. Link a simple portfolio or demo video. Thank them, and don’t pressure for a reply.

4) Make it easy to help

Share a one‑pager before the call with context, goals, and questions. After the chat, send a thank‑you and a short summary of what you’ll do next. This shows maturity and encourages mentors to stay involved.

Screenshot placeholder — concise outreach email with clear ask
Specific, polite outreach gets better responses than generic mass messages.

Build a simple portfolio that grows with you

Collect 2–3 short project write‑ups on a single page: goal, your role, link to a demo or repo, and what you learned. Add one paragraph on how the project connects to local needs in Abu Dhabi, such as recycling education or water conservation. Update this page after each event or mentorship session.

Safety, ethics, and permissions

  • Follow school policies, get permission for photos, and protect privacy.
  • Be transparent about data: collect the minimum needed; avoid sensitive info.
  • Respect time: mentors are volunteers — arrive prepared and finish on time.
