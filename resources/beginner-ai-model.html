Beginner AI: First Model in 30 Minutes

Back to Hub
AI 6–7 min read

Training your first AI model doesn’t require advanced math or expensive hardware. With student‑friendly tools like Google’s Teachable Machine — or visual coding platforms like Scratch extensions — you can build a tiny prototype in under an hour and, more importantly, learn the habits of safe, honest experimentation.

Teachable Machine lets you collect images, train, and test in your browser.

Why this matters for youth in Abu Dhabi

AI is shaping how we learn, work, and solve problems. Building a simple model helps students understand how data quality, labeling, and evaluation affect outcomes. It also opens a path to apply technology to local challenges — from recycling education to campus safety — in ways that are ethical, age‑appropriate, and aligned with school policies.

Project: a “Hello World” image classifier

Goal: Train a tiny model to distinguish between two categories (for example, recyclable vs. non‑recyclable items, or two kinds of school objects). You’ll create a dataset, train, test, and reflect on limitations.

1) Define the task and guardrails

Pick a harmless, school‑safe topic. Avoid any sensitive or identifiable personal data. Agree on ground rules with a teacher: no faces, no private info, and always ask permission before taking photos. Decide the two classes you’ll recognize and write down 1–2 simple use cases (e.g., a demo that shows which bin to use).

2) Collect a tiny dataset

Capture 20–50 images per class using different angles, lighting, and backgrounds. Keep it balanced (similar number of examples for each class). Label images clearly. Variety matters more than quantity at this stage. If photographing objects, place them on a simple background to reduce noise.

3) Train in Teachable Machine

Create an image project, add two classes, and upload your images. Use default settings for the first run. Click Train and wait a minute. When done, explore the preview webcam or image upload to test your model. Note your accuracy, but also pay attention to where it misclassifies.

A simple loop: collect a bit of data, train, test, and reflect.

4) Test honestly and record results

Test on new images that the model hasn’t seen before. Vary lighting and background to see where it fails. Capture 5–10 test cases and write a short note: what worked, what didn’t, and why. Avoid exaggerated claims — small models make mistakes, and that’s okay when you’re learning.

5) Improve the dataset

Add examples for challenging cases (e.g., crumpled wrappers, reflective bottles). Keep classes balanced. Try training again and compare results. Discuss whether improvements are due to better data, different angles, or simply randomness in training.

6) Share responsibly

Present a short demo with three parts: what you tried, what the model can do, and what it cannot do. Include a note on privacy and ethics. If you export the model, do so only for harmless demos (like a sorting game). Clearly state that the project is educational, not a production system.

Optional: try Scratch + AI extensions

If you prefer a block‑based approach, use Scratch with computer vision extensions to create interactive projects (e.g., a game that reacts to an object). The learning goals are the same: be thoughtful about data, test honestly, and reflect on what you learned.

Where to go next

  • Increase classes (e.g., paper, plastic, metal) and compare difficulty.
  • Write a 1‑page reflection covering data quality, overfitting, and ethics.
  • Connect to a community use case, like labeling school bins with icons.
